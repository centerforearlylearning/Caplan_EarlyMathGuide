Developmental Milestone

Children at this point in development are beginning to understand how the numbers in the count follow a structured order, much like they learn the ABCs. The goal at this stage is to help children learn to remember the order of the count list by providing fun and easy to remember examples, such as counting songs. Similarly, counting games can help children understand the structure of the counting routine and how each number word applies to one item when counting. There are many ways to promote children’s early counting skills. Here we have examples of songs and games that can be used to help promote children’s learning.

Games

Goal: Games can provide a fun way to practice counting. For board games, look for ones that allow children to move along the board in a relatively straight line (e.g., chutes and ladders). To help children develop an understanding of how to count from 1-to-10, have children count out the number of spots they move and have them watch while you do the same. For card games, look for cards that have a countable set (e.g., playing cards) and ask children to count the smaller numbers to find out how many there are.

Examples (click the games with links to find how to play!):

  • Chutes and ladders
  • Candy land
  • Hop scotch
  • Memory Match (card game)

Songs

Goal: Songs provide a fun introduction to the counting routine. The tune from the music provides cues to help children remember the order of the counting sequence. Find a song that you and your child would like to sing and see if you can learn the tune and repeat it later on!

Examples:

  • Sesame Street:1, 2, 4, 4 (2.5 minute video)
  • Sesame Street: Learn to count to 5 (10 minute video)
  • One, two, buckle my shoe (1 minute, short video)

Other helpful activities

  • Check out this fun guide for how to play number card games with your child
  • Counting body parts while getting dressed or during diaper changes, counting kisses, food pieces, pictures in books
  • Counting while getting dressed (e.g., one arm goes into the jacket, and now your second arm goes into your jacket); when eating (e.g., handing blueberries to child one at a time and count them aloud); hands and feet, fingers and toes while changing and during bath time; steps to the front door
  • Counting: while cleaning (e.g., picking up toys/trash), categories of things (e.g., counting all green things), at traffic light, going up stairs, backwards on the calendar, making up stories that include counting parts of animals, down to get ready to transition to another activity
