Beginner Counters

Children at this point in development are beginning to understand how the numbers in the count follow a structured order, much like they learn the ABCs. The goal at this stage is to help children gain familiarity with important math concepts using different number games and songs. Below are different activities we have compiled with the help of early childcare experts. These activities and suggestions are meant to be a starting point or framework for you and your child to explore. Adapt or change the activities to find out what works best for you and your child. The best activities are those that you and your child enjoy doing and want to do again!

To help guide your understanding of your child's mathematical skills based off your answers on the previous page, we have also included an overview of your child's progress towards the bottom of this page.

Games

Games are essential for teaching children to count because they provide an interactive and enjoyable context, making learning more engaging and less intimidating for young children. Both board and card games can help reinforce and consolidate mathematical concepts and foster a positive attitude towards mathematics.

Songs

Songs provide a fun introduction to the counting routine. The tune from the music provides cues to help children remember the order of the counting sequence. Find a song that you and your child would like to sing and see if you can learn the tune and repeat it later on!

Storybooks

Books can provide a great way to help children learn about set size. At this stage, children may be able to identify how many there are on a page by remembering what you have read or by looking at the numeral on the page.

Verbal Counting

Children at this point in development are beginning to understand how the numbers in the count follow a structured order, much like they learn the ABCs. The goal at this stage is to help children learn to remember the order of the count list by providing fun and easy to remember examples, such as counting songs. Similarly, counting games can help children understand the structure of the counting routine and how each number word applies to one item when counting. There are many ways to promote children’s early counting skills. Above we have examples of songs and games that can be used to help promote children’s learning.

Cardinality

Children’s understanding of counting and cardinality (the total number of items in a set) initially develop separately. Early on, children’s understanding of counting develops much like their understanding of the ABCs – they know the order the words go in but they do not yet know the set size that each number word goes to. At this point in development children are beginning to recognize small set sizes without needing to count. For example, seeing two crackers and knowing there are two without needing to count. However, they may struggle to regularly use counting to answer questions about the total. There are several ways to help children understand how to use counting to identify the total number of items, including using counting books and games that require children to identify how many items there are.

Story Problems

Once children have developed an understanding of counting and set size (cardinality), they can begin to understand how to combine and decompose sets. Initially, children will be able to show an understanding of simple arithmetic using manipulatives such as blocks. For example, understanding that adding one item and then removing one item leaves the total number the same. Eventually, children link this understanding to their understanding of mathematical symbols. The symbolic understanding of mathematics that comes from knowing how to count and recognizing numerals allows children to perform the more advanced arithmetic they will learn at school.

Number Naming

At this point in development, children may begin to recognize a few numerals that they regularly see. However, for unfamiliar numerals, particularly those beyond three or four, children have difficulty consistently identifying the number. In part, this is due to children regularly seeing numerals for smaller numbers or numbers that match their age (e.g., a three year old seeing 3 or being asked about 3). To help strengthen children's understanding of these mathematical symbols, regular exposure and use of numerals is necessary. Fortunately, there are many fun activities, such as board and card games that allow children to show off their number naming skills!
