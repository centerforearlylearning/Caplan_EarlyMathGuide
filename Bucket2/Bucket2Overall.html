Early Counters

Children at this point in development have an understanding of the initial structure of the count list, and are learning to extend this to higher numbers. This understanding is relatively rigid, with children knowing how to count starting from “one” but not yet understanding how counting can be applied in varied ways. Below are different activities we have compiled with the help of early childcare experts. These activities and suggestions are meant to be a starting point or framework for you and your child to explore. Adapt or change the activities to find out what works best for you and your child. The best activities are those that you and your child enjoy doing and want to do again!

To help guide your understanding of your child's mathematical skills based off your answers on the previous page, we have also included an overview of your child's progress towards the bottom of this page.

Board and card games

Providing children with a fun and low-stakes context for talking about numbers can be one of the most effective ways to boost their math skills! Both board and card games can help reinforce and consolidate mathematical concepts and foster a positive attitude towards mathematics.

Number songs

Songs can help children remember the the larger structure of counting and how to count to higher numbers. A catchy tune can be the reminder your child needs when they get stuck on what number comes next!

Counting books

Books can help children learn the concepts behind counting. At this stage, children have an understanding of how to count for small numbers, but they are still learning the ways in which counting can or cannot tell you how many there are.

Verbal Counting

Children at this point in development have an understanding of the initial structure of the count list, and are learning to extend this to higher numbers. This understanding is relatively rigid, with children knowing how to count starting from “one” but not yet understanding how counting can be applied in varied ways.

Cardinality

Children’s understanding of counting and cardinality (the total number of items in a set) initially develop separately. Early on, children’s understanding of counting develops much like their understanding of the ABCs – they know the order the words go in but they do not yet know the set size that each number word goes to. At this point in development children are beginning to recognize small set sizes without needing to count and may be able to use counting to determine how many there are in sets fewer than five. However, they may struggle to regularly use counting to answer questions about the total, particulary for larger set sizes. There are several ways to help children understand how to use counting to identify the total number of items, including using counting books and games that require children to identify how many items there are.

Story Problems

At this point in development children are still developing an understanding of how many items are in a set. Initially, children will be able to show an understanding of simple arithmetic using manipulatives such as blocks. For example, understanding that adding one item and then removing one item leaves the total number the same. Eventually, children link this understanding to their understanding of mathematical symbols. The symbolic understanding of mathematics that comes from knowing how to count and recognizing numerals allows children to perform the more advanced arithmetic they will learn at school.

Number Naming

Children's understanding of numerals develops as they gain more and more familiarity with seeing and identifying numbers. At this point in development children have the most difficulty with unfamiliar numbers, particularly those beyond 5. In part, this is due to children regularly seeing numerals for smaller numbers or numbers that match their age (e.g., a four year old seeing 4 or being asked about 4) as well as children's experience succesfully counting sets up to five. To help strengthen children's understanding of these mathematical symbols, regular exposure and use of numerals is necessary. Fortunately, there are many fun activities, such as board and card games that allow children to show off their number naming skills!
