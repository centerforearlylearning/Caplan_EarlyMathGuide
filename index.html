Verbal Counting

Children's verbal counting skills provide the basis for their understanding of more formal mathematics concepts. Early on, children will learn to count much like they learn their ABCs. They understand these number words go in a particular order, and they may even have a song to help them remember! However, it is not until later on that they begin to connect counting to the numbers those words represent (see "Cardinality" below). Through practice and regular exposure to opportunities to exercise their counting skills, children learn the patterns of how to count to increasingly higher numbers.

Cardinality

Childrenâ€™s understanding of counting and cardinality (the total number of items in a set) initially develop separately. Early on in development, children can identify how many items there are for small sets without needing to count. Adults do this too! For example, you can look at three candies and know, without needing to count, that there are exactly three. However, for larger sets you will need to count to figure out how many there are. For children, once they begin to recognize the connection between counting and cardinality (i.e., knowing that if they count 1-2-3-4-5 that means there are five in all), they unlock the ability to use counting in meaningful way in their everyday life.

Through the use of counting games and books, caregivers can help children learn how counting is used to understand how many there are.

Story Problems

Once children have developed a conceptual understanding of counting and cardinality they're better able to follow along and help contribute to mathematical stories. Younger children can follow along and show an understanding of simple arithmetic using phsyical objects. For example, they may be able to tell you that if you have three blocks adding and then taking away two will leave you with the same amount. As children gain more experience with story problems using manipulatives their understanding strengthens and they can follow along by keeping track of the numbers and operations on their fingers or in their own head.

Number Naming

Understanding the symbols for different numbers is an important milestone for children's early mathematical development. Regular exposure to, and use of, numerals helps support children's attention towards these mathematical symbols. Many children will first focus on numerals that are particularly important to their own lives, like the number that corresponds to their current age. Like verbal counting, children's understanding of numerals first develops separately from their understanding of how the number refers to a specific set size. For example, a child may recognize that "five" is the name for 5 before they would be able to give you five items if you were to ask. Eventually, children gain experience seeing the numeral, hearing the name for that numeral, and also seeing the set size that corresponds to the numeral that they make the connection across these different reprentations -- forming a more complete understanding of numbers!
