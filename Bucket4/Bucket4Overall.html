Consistent Counters

Children at this point in development have a solid grasp on the basics of counting and are developing a more advanced understanding that allows them to see the patterns involved in the counting process. Children can consistently count up to 20 and may be discovering the repeating pattern withing each decade (e.g., 20, 30, 40), allowing them quickly learn how to count up to 100. Below are different activities we have compiled with the help of early childcare experts. These activities and suggestions are meant to be a starting point or framework for you and your child to explore. Adapt or change the activities to find out what works best for you and your child. The best activities are those that you and your child enjoy doing and want to do again!

Verbal Counting

Children at this point in development have a solid grasp on the basics of counting and are developing a more advanced understanding that allows them to see the patterns involved in the counting process. Children can consistently count up to 20 and may be discovering the repeating pattern withing each decade (e.g., 20, 30, 40), allowing them quickly learn how to count up to 100. By developing an advanced understanding of the structure of the count list, children may be able to more flexibly engage with the mathematics in their environment.

Cardinality

Children’s understanding of counting and cardinality (the total number of items in a set) initially develop separately. Early on, children’s understanding of counting develops much like their understanding of the ABC's – they know the order the words go in but they do not yet know the set size that each number word goes to. At this point in development children have started to make this connection and should be able to tell you how many there are after counting. For example, being asked to count a set of eight toy cars and telling you there are eight. However, children may still struggle with consistently answering how many there are when it involves larger sets or when it involves having to count out only a subset of items (e.g., can you count out all the yellow M&Ms and tell me how many there are?). Such counting requires coordination children are not used to needing, making it more difficult for them to follow along and keep track of how many there are. There are several ways to help children understand how to coordinate counting to identify larger sets, including using counting books and incorporating cardinality questions into everyday activities!

Story Problems

Once children have developed an understanding of counting and set size (cardinality), they can begin to understand how to combine and decompose sets. At this point in development children will rely less on needing use physical objects to keep track of what is being asked. Instead they can begin to mentally represent the numbers being discussed (e.g., knowing that if there are three kids playing and two more were to join, then there would be five kids in all). Being able to keep track of such questions and story problems shows a more advanced understanding of mathematics that sets children up well for their time in school.

Number Naming

At this point in development, children may show a bigger interest in numbers, wanting to show off all the numerals they know! This understanding of how their number words connect to the numerals they provides children with a symbolic understanding of mathematics. Although children may know many different numerals, they will still struggle to name numbers they are unfamiliar with and will still need help linking these numerals to their relative set. For example, once children can identify 10 and 8, they may still need help to understand that 10 is more than 8. To help strengthen children's understanding of these mathematical symbols, regular exposure and use of numerals is necessary as well as regular comparison of different numerals. Fortunately, there are many fun activities, such as board and card games that allow children to show off their number naming and comparison skills!
